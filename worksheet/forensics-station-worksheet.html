What is digital forensics?

~3 min

Digital forensics is the science of finding facts on phones, computers, and drives — then proving them. A good investigator never trusts one clue. They collect several pieces of evidence and check whether the story holds together.

Metadata — hidden data about a file: when a photo was taken, and the GPS location where the phone was.
Timeline — putting events in time order to see what really happened, and when.
Deleted ≠ gone — "deleted" files often sit in a Recently Deleted folder and can be recovered.
Corroboration — when two or more clues point to the same fact, you can trust it.
Q1. In your own words, why shouldn't an investigator trust a single screenshot or one photo by itself?

Your assignment

~2 min
Case Brief

Dana Brisco, a clerk at Hartwell Hardware in the town of Brindle Falls, called in sick last Saturday. Their manager suspects Dana faked it to attend the sold-out Aurora Static concert. Dana handed over their phone to clear their name. Your job: examine the phone and decide — does the alibi hold up?

How to work

Open every app on the phone — Messages, Phone, Photos, Browser, and Notes — and check each Recently Deleted folder. Don't stop at the first thing you find. Record what you see in the evidence log on the next page.

Q2. Before you start: what would Dana's phone look like if they really were home sick all day? Write one prediction.
